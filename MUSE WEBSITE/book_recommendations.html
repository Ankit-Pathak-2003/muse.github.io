The Bell Jar

Posted on October 17, 2022

The Bell Jar reads like a confession, yet for Sylvia Plath, there is no sense of salvation. Through Esther Greenwood, Plath writes a revelatory narrative drawing from her obsessive-compulsive tendencies and quiet suicidal ideations. The Bell Jar is all the more poignant, since it was her only foray into novel form, published just a month before her death.

God of small things

Posted on October 17, 2022

'The secret of being a bore is to tell everything.' Voltaire said. This book is an appropriate example of how true that adage is. Like a loving mother with only one piece of pie, she withholds information and doles it out at the most opportune moments, yet never does the plot become incomprehensible. In fact, we lap it all up and can't wait for the next serving. To even attempt to summarize the plot would be to take everything away from it because, well, surprise!, the book really is about the Small Things. And the Really Big Things.

For Whom The Bell Tolls

Posted on November 21, 2022

Narratives about war often fall into a trap of inadvertently advertising the subject they are meant to criticize. The inevitable spectacle, the bleak human drama, the morose tragedy and the grand displays of the sounds and the furies that war brings give them an almost a romantic, albeit farcical tinge. Hemingway defies such pretensions. Hemingway portrays the Spanish Civil War with such deft acuteness, such heightened sense of reality that to experience For Whom the Bells Toll is to witness all the horrors that man inflicts.
